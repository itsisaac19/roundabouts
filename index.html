THESIS STATEMENT
Roundabouts redefine how we move through cities by improving safety, reducing congestion, and creating a more efficient flow of traffic compared to traditional intersections.
What are Conflict Points?
A conflict point is defined as any spot where two vehicles' paths can intersect, merge, or diverge. A traditional 4-way single-lane intersection has 32 vehicle to vehicle conflict points.
Crossing Conflicts
These occur where traffic streams intersect at right angles, such as when one car drives straight through while another turns across its path. They create the highest risk for deadly T-bone crashes, which strike vehicles directly from the side. Because drivers often approach at full speed, these collisions are violent and frequently fatal.
Merging Conflicts
These happen when one lane of traffic joins another moving in the same direction, like when a driver turns right into ongoing traffic. While less severe than crossing crashes, they can still cause sideswipes or rear-end collisions when drivers misjudge gaps or fail to yield.
Diverging Conflicts
These arise when one stream of traffic splits into two, such as when a vehicle slows to turn off the main road. Rear-end crashes are common here, as following drivers may not react quickly enough. Although frequent, these collisions are usually minor compared to other types.
